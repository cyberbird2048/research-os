## A. Executive Answer

**Primary question (HIS-002 brief §1).** During the buildout of US electricity generation and grid infrastructure and the electrification of industrial manufacturing (c. 1900–1935), how did social-value creation, infrastructure capital cycles, financing structure, and investor entry timing affect who created value, who captured returns, and who suffered impairment?

The evidence supports a direct answer only if five strata are held rigorously apart. They are reported below as separate ledgers; the study deliberately does **not** collapse them into a single "utility-sector" figure.

**1. Social / downstream value creation — real, measurable, but localized and contested.** Industrial electrification is associated with a substantial early manufacturing-productivity signal: a ~4% cross-industry productivity differential by 1900 rising to ~11% by 1920 and holding to 1940, with +1% electric-HP/value-added associated with +1.5% labor productivity by 1920 (E-102); by 1920 a 75th-vs-25th-percentile energy-intensity industry near hydropower shows ~10% faster labor-productivity growth, with gains appearing from 1900 (E-101). A broad-economy productivity acceleration coexists in the tail of the window (1929–1941 MFP ≈2.27–2.36%/yr, the highest peacetime peak-to-peak of the century — E-108). But the signal is **localized to hydro-proximate counties and slow at the national level** (E-107); the authors themselves concede it "may have been largely" cheaper-hydro energy access rather than electrification-as-general-purpose-technology (E-106); and in concentrated markets the measured productivity gain looks more like higher **markups** than diffuse consumer pass-through (E-105). **Confidence: medium; the social-value stratum is real but bounded, geographically concentrated, and partly attributable to inputs other than electrification per se.**

**2. Operating-company economics — largely a gap in the open evidence.** Direct operating-company income data (electricity revenue, generating cost per kWh, reported operating profit) was **not obtained** on the open-source scope; the FTC full text that would carry it (S-005) is deferred (§H). What exists is (i) *physical* operating-group evidence — nine investor-owned holding-company operating groups generated ~18% of 1930 US output, ~24% of it moving interstate (E-215), a broader eleven-group tally >19% of output (E-216); and (ii) a *securities-return* proxy at the operating tier (Cowles C-11), which is an investor-return measure, not an operating-economics measure, and is reported under stratum 3. **Confidence: n/a — operating-company profitability is an explicit evidence gap, not a finding.**

**3. Holding-company (and operating-tier) investor outcomes — large 1920s gains, deep collapse, worst at the holding tier.** Utility-securities investors saw electric/gas **holding**-tier total return compound +1426% and **operating**-tier +550% from Dec-1921 to Dec-1929, against +291% for all stocks (E-114) — then a collapse: from the Sep-1929 peak to Dec-1935, operating −61.8%, holding −81.1%, all-stocks −42.9% (E-115). Peak-to-trough (Sep-1929→Jun-1932), holding fell −89.8% vs operating −74.6% (E-301). The holding tier was both the biggest 1920s gainer and the biggest loser. **Boundary (fixed): at the index level this entire tier dispersion is absorbed by market beta (E-307); it is reported as an outcome, not as proof of a financing-structure mechanism.** **Confidence: high for the return magnitudes (within-series ratios, Cowles caveats in force); the *cause* of the tier gap is not established at index level.**

**4. Physical infrastructure deployment — a large, demand-tracking buildout, not a demonstrated national overbuild.** Installed capacity rose 2.35× from 1920 to 1929 (12.7M→29.8M kW) and output peaked at 92.2B kWh in 1929 (E-201, E-203), overwhelmingly investor-owned (93–95% in-window — E-202). Through the 1920s capacity and output grew at essentially the same rate (≈+9.9%/yr each), with flat utilization (E-210) — i.e., **no pre-Depression utilization erosion from overbuilding.** Utilization did fall −25.3% from 1929 to 1932 (E-207), but that drop decomposes to ≈55% Depression demand shock and ≈45% continued (pipeline) capacity additions (E-208); within the core window, output had recovered above its 1929 peak by 1935 and utilization had materially recovered (E-203, E-209), and the 1937 capacity factor of 38.1% — read only as **post-window sensitivity** — shows further recovery (E-206). There is **no evidence of persistent national utilization impairment through the available post-window sensitivity.** **Confidence: high; the "national physical overbuild in the 1920s" reading is not supported (fixed boundary 2).**

**5. Entry timing and financing structure — a real dispersion whose mechanism the open evidence cannot resolve.** Two same-sector tiers behaved very differently — a ~0.5 beta gap (operating β≈0.86 vs holding β≈1.38 — E-308) and a 15.2pp deeper holding drawdown (E-301) — a pattern qualitatively consistent with holding-company pyramiding/leverage and illustrated by named distress cases (§F). **But the index-level dispersion is fully explained by static market beta with no residual (E-307), and Cowles carries no security identities, issuance vintages, capital structures, or delisting/liquidation records (E-311). The security-level financing/entry mechanism is therefore `Undecidable-pending-access` (E-370).** **Confidence: the dispersion is high-confidence as a fact; its attribution to financing structure is undecidable on current evidence (fixed boundary 3).**

**Why outcomes differed (held at the descriptive level).** The evidence shows social/productivity value and utility-securities investor experience **coexisting and diverging** across the window — high measured productivity (E-101, E-102, E-108) alongside a securities collapse concentrated at the holding tier (E-115, E-301) — but it does **not** show that the value created by specific electrified assets did or did not reach the specific investors who financed them; that matched-cohort question is undecidable (E-118). The physical infrastructure kept delivering (output recovered above its 1929 peak by 1935, E-203, with utilization materially recovered within the core window, E-209, and further recovery visible in the 1937 post-window sensitivity, E-206) even as holding-tier investors were deeply impaired. Technological and social importance did not translate into a uniform investor return, and the two must not be equated.

## B. Historical Timeline (United States, c. 1900–1935)

*Only the mechanics needed for the value question are covered; this is not a general history of US electrification. Physical figures are from the Historical Statistics Chapter-G series (S-001), corroborated at 1902 by the Census of Electrical Industries (S-002); securities figures are from the Cowles series (S-008); the 1912 census scope break is not spliced (E-217).*

**Central-station generation, 1902–1920.** From the earliest comparable benchmark, US central-station output rose from 2.5B kWh (1902) to 39.4B kWh (1920), and installed capacity from ~1.2M kW (1902) to 12.7M kW (1920) (QB-2). Ownership was investor-dominated from the outset: the 1902 Census puts private dynamo capacity at 90.6% and private output at 92.2% (E-213, E-214), figures that reconcile to the unit with the Historical Statistics ownership split for 1902 (E-213). Utilization (production per kW) rose from a 23.6% capacity factor (1902) toward 35.4% (1920) (E-206), i.e., the early grid was being used more intensively as it scaled.

**Grid buildout and industrial electrification, 1900–1929.** The demand-side/social-value layer — factory electrification — shows a productivity differential building from ~4% (1900) to ~11% (1920) and persisting to 1940 (E-102), concentrated near hydropower and in energy-intensive industries (E-101, E-107). Capacity expansion accelerated through the 1920s: 2.35× from 1920 to 1929 (E-201), with output growing at essentially the same ≈+9.9%/yr rate as capacity and utilization roughly flat (E-210). Within the decade there was a mid-decade capacity wave — 1923→1925 capacity +37.3% vs output +20.0%, then 1925→1929 output +50.0% vs capacity +39.0% as demand caught up (E-211).

**Utility holding-company expansion, 1920s.** Investor-owned operating utilities were increasingly organized under multi-layer holding-company groups. The bounded evidence documents this structurally rather than financially: nine investor-owned holding-company operating groups generated ~18% of 1930 US output with ~24% moving interstate (E-215), and a broader eleven-group tally exceeded 19% of output with ~25% interstate (E-216). Named 1928–29 holding vehicles in the security-level micro-sample include Middle West Utilities (Insull), American & Foreign Power and Electric Bond & Share (EBASCO) (E-353, E-354, E-356). *The internal financial mechanics of these groups (inter-affiliate leverage, pyramiding, service contracts) are not established by the open evidence — see §E and §H.*

**The 1929–1932 collapse.** Utility-securities returns peaked in Sep-1929 and collapsed to a Jun-1932 trough: holding-tier total return −89.8%, operating −74.6% (E-301); the broad market (all-stocks) fell −82.3% over the same window, so the holding tier was worse than the market and the operating tier shallower (E-302). Physically, output fell from its 92.2B kWh peak (1929) to 79.4B kWh (1932) while capacity kept rising +15.2% (no retirements) (E-203, E-204); utilization fell −25.3% (capacity factor 35.3%→26.4%) (E-207), of which ≈55% was output falling on fixed plant (demand shock) and ≈45% continued capacity additions (E-208). The stress was concentrated in the investor-owned sector (private output −14.9% vs public +5.1%, 1929→1932) (E-212).

**Recovery through 1935 (core window; 1937 as post-window sensitivity).** Within the core window, output recovered to 95.3B kWh by 1935 — above its 1929 peak (E-203) — and utilization materially recovered, up +19.8% from 1932 to 1935 on an essentially fixed capacity base (E-209); recovery was demand-led, not achieved by scrapping "excess" plant. Read only as **post-window sensitivity**, the 1932→1937 figures extend the picture — capacity near-frozen (+3.6%) while output grew +49.8% (E-205), the capacity factor reaching 38.1% by 1937 (E-206). Securities recovered only partially: six-year cumulative total return from Dec-1929 to Dec-1935 was −40.5% (operating) and −66.5% (holding) (E-303). The core window closes in 1935 — an endpoint adopted as a **scope convention** from Brief §2.2 (the Public Utility Holding Company Act), not a ledger-derived historical conclusion.

## C. Value Creation vs. Value Capture

*The brief (§7) and the fixed boundaries require three measures be kept distinct and never merged into a single value figure: (i) productivity / social value; (ii) operating-company economics; (iii) securities investor return.*

**(i) Productivity / social value.** The load-bearing source (S-018) establishes a genuine, quantified industrial-electrification productivity effect: ~4%→~11% cross-industry differential 1900→1920, held to 1940 (E-102), and ~10% faster growth for high-energy-intensity industries near hydropower by 1920 (E-101). This is corroborated in the tail by a broad-economy MFP acceleration (E-108). Three qualifications bound it: the effect is **localized** (hydro-proximate counties; the national signal was slow — E-107); it is **partly not electrification per se** (the authors' cheaper-hydro hedge — E-106); and where markets were concentrated the productivity gain reads as **markups rather than diffuse pass-through**, so a high productivity coefficient is not a clean proxy for broad social value (E-105). The gains also accrued disproportionately to capital (a negative labor-share effect in electrified energy-intensive cells — E-103) and came more from output than employment growth, with "new jobs … on net lower paying" (E-104). **Conclusion (medium confidence): real but bounded, geographically concentrated, capital-weighted social value.**

**(ii) Operating-company economics.** This measure is largely **absent** from the open evidence and must not be inferred from either the productivity series or the securities series. The ledger carries only *physical* operating-group data (output share and interstate flow — E-215, E-216) and no operating-company income statements (revenue, cost per kWh, reported operating profit); the source that would carry them (FTC, S-005) is deferred (§H). The Cowles "operating" tier (C-11) is a *securities-return* series for operating-company equities — an investor-return measure — and is reported under (iii), not here. **Conclusion: operating-company profitability is an explicit gap, not a finding.**

**(iii) Securities investor return.** Utility-securities investors earned very large 1920s gains and suffered a deep collapse (E-114, E-115, E-301, E-303; full magnitudes in §A.3 and §E). This is a distinct measure from both (i) and (ii): it reflects market pricing of utility equities, not the productivity of electrified factories or the operating economics of the utilities themselves.

**The divergence, stated within the fixed boundary.** Placing (i) beside (iii), high measured social/productivity value **coexists** in 1900–1935 with weak, uneven, and (post-1929) sharply negative utility-securities returns (E-113). This is recorded as **coexisting divergence only**. No source here links a cohort of assets' measured social value to the same cohort's investor return; the matched-cohort value-transfer question — whether the value created by specific electrified assets accrued to the investors who financed them — is **Undecidable with current evidence** (E-118), and the Rejects test (no divergence at either tier in an overlapping window) was executed and **not established** (E-117). The two ledgers must not be merged into one value measure.

## D. Capital Cycle and Physical Capacity

*Per fixed boundary 2, this section rests only on capacity/output/utilization evidence; equity-price decline and holding-company collapse are excluded as overbuild proxies (E-218 guardrail; brief §8.2).*

**1920s expansion.** Capacity rose 2.35× 1920→1929 (E-201), but so did demand: capacity CAGR ≈+9.9%/yr essentially equaled output CAGR ≈+9.9%/yr, with utilization roughly flat across the decade (E-210). There was a mid-decade capacity wave (1923→1925 capacity ran ahead of output) that demand then absorbed by 1929 (E-211) — a transient slack, not a sustained oversupply. On the physical indicators the brief pre-specified for PAT-002 (capacity vs demand/utilization; declining utilization; underused capacity), **the 1920s do not show a national overbuild.**

**1929–1932 utilization decline — demand shock vs pipeline capacity.** Utilization fell −25.3% (capacity factor 35.3%→26.4%) 1929→1932 (E-207). Decomposed, ≈55% of the −780 kWh/kW drop is output falling on fixed capacity (a Depression **demand** shock) and ≈45% is continued capacity additions (E-208). Capacity kept rising +15.2% into the demand collapse with no retirements (E-204) — a genuine physical-slack signature, but one reflecting **pre-1929 pipeline commitments**, not a fresh 1930s oversupply decision. The stress was concentrated in the investor-owned segment (private output −14.9% vs public +5.1%, 1929→1932 — E-212), consistent with the brief's expectation that any overbuild-like pattern might be narrower than the whole sector.

**1932–1935 recovery (core window; 1937 as post-window sensitivity).** Recovery was **demand-led, not achieved by scrapping capacity**: within the core window, utilization recovered +19.8% from 1932 to 1935 on an essentially fixed capacity base (E-209) and output rose above its 1929 peak by 1935 (E-203). Read only as post-window sensitivity, the 1932→1937 figures extend this — capacity near-frozen (+3.6%) while output grew +49.8% (E-205), the capacity factor reaching 38.1% by 1937 (E-206). There was real slack in 1932, but the 1920s-built plant was **substantially reabsorbed by 1935, with further recovery visible in the 1937 post-window sensitivity**; there is **no evidence of persistent national utilization impairment through the available post-window sensitivity.**

**Net (within fixed boundary 2).** A large, demand-tracking buildout, a mid-decade transient capacity wave, and a 1929–1932 slack that is majority Depression demand shock plus pre-1929 pipeline capacity — **not a demonstrated US 1920s national physical overbuild.**

## E. Financing Structure and Investor Outcomes

*Per fixed boundary 3 and brief §6/§7: operating-company securities, holding-company securities, market beta, and firm/security-level evidence are kept distinct.*

**Operating-company securities (Cowles C-11).** The operating tier compounded +550% Dec-1921→Dec-1929 (E-114), fell −74.6% peak-to-trough (E-301), and was −40.5% cumulatively Dec-1929→Dec-1935 (E-303). Notably, operating was **more defensive than the market** in the crash (−74.6% vs all-stocks −82.3% — E-302) with a below-market beta (β≈0.86 — E-308) — undercutting a simple "utilities crashed harder" story.

**Holding-company securities (Cowles C-16).** The holding tier compounded +1426% Dec-1921→Dec-1929 (E-114), fell −89.8% peak-to-trough (E-301) — 15.2pp deeper than operating — and was −66.5% cumulatively to Dec-1935 (E-303). Price-only (ex-dividend) figures confirm the tier gap (operating −77.7% vs holding −90.7%), so **dividends are not what creates the gap** (E-305). The holding tier was a poor long-run compounder *before* the 1920s (flat-to-down 1900–1913 — E-116), cautioning against reading the 1921–29 surge as typical.

**Market beta — the load-bearing control.** The holding tier's −89.8% drawdown is **fully explained by static market beta**: its β (1.38 full-window / 1.46 in the crash) applied to the market's drawdown predicts −90.8% to −92.0%, equal to or slightly worse than actual, leaving **no positive residual** for a distinct financing-structure crash at the index level (E-307, E-306). The ~0.5 beta gap between tiers (E-308) is itself the market-exposure difference, not a residual after controlling for it. **Because leverage is one source of high beta, "beta explains it" does not exclude a financing-structure mechanism — it shows only that Cowles cannot isolate one** (E-307). The aggregate utilities index (C-4) sits between the tiers and masks the dispersion (E-310).

**Firm/security-level evidence — bounded sample only, the rest undecidable.** A capped 5-issuer, 2-issue CFC micro-sample (QB-3) gives real security-level prices and one distress signal set (§F), but Cowles is index-level only (no security identities, issuance vintages, capital structures, leverage ratios, or delistings — E-311), and the CFC sample cannot establish issuance vintage, capital-structure tier, authoritative operating-vs-holding tiering (e.g., the hybrid Public Service Corp. of New Jersey — E-355), delisting dates, liquidation outcomes, population-scale reconstruction, security-level total return, or dividend continuity (E-370). **The security-level financing/entry mechanism is therefore `Undecidable-pending-access`.** Additional measurement limits push the same way: the value-weighted, changing-composition, chained indexes embed **survivorship**, so true holding-company investor loss (including total wipeouts) is *understated* by the index drawdown (E-309).

## F. Failure and Control Cases

*The brief's failure-case quota (§5) requires named impairment cases; the fixed boundaries require these be read as illustrative distress, not as a proven population mechanism.*

**Holding-company distress (bounded CFC sample).**
- **American & Foreign Power common (holding, EBASCO):** ≈80 (early Jan 1929) → ≈7 (early Jan 1932) — a same-issuer comparand showing an ~91% price decline over three years (E-354, E-357).
- **Electric Bond & Share common (holding, EBASCO):** a CY1928 high near 180⅞ → ≈10¾–11⅞ in early Jan 1932 (E-356, E-359).
- **Middle West Utilities (holding, Insull):** by Jan-1932 it declared its quarterly common dividend **payable in common stock** (a stock dividend, not cash) and offered its $6 preferred $1.50 cash *or* 3/80ths of a common share — a cash-conservation/distress signal (E-360); its 5% bonds due 1932/1935 traded at 55/38 per 100 par (Dec-1931), senior-debt-tier distress pricing (E-361). *These are distress signals, not a liquidation outcome: the Insull collapse of April–June 1932 post-dates the Jan-1932 issue and is not in the open evidence (E-370).*

**Operating-company comparison (control).**
- **Commonwealth Edison (operating, Insull):** ~207–219 (early Jan 1929) → 115–118 (early Jan 1932) (E-352, E-358), and it was still **declaring its regular $2.00 quarterly dividend** in early 1929 (E-351). Set beside American & Foreign Power (~91% decline) and Middle West Utilities (dividend paid in stock; bonds at 38–55), the operating issuer's ~45% price decline with a maintained cash dividend is a qualitatively milder outcome — consistent with, but not proof of, the tier dispersion seen at the index level.

**Stated limitation.** This is a **5-issuer sample, not a population-scale security-level reconstruction.** Reliable table extraction from the rough-OCR CFC volumes is partially defeated for the columnar NYSE "Stock Record" and "Dividends Declared" tables; the readable NY Curb records and prose reviews are the source of these cases (E-370). The absence of a population-scale, vintage-resolved security-level dataset is a first-order limitation on any financing-structure conclusion (§H).

## G. Counter-Evidence and Alternative Explanations

*Per the brief's Anti-Confirmation Rule (§8.4) and Counter-Evidence Requirements (§4), the alternatives below are given equal standing with any pattern-consistent reading. The ledger's Anti-Confirmation Balance Check found each Pattern's Weakens/Rejects/Undecidable lanes are not weaker than its Supports lane — and for the physical and financing questions, they dominate.*

- **Cheaper hydropower and geographic selection.** The productivity effect "may have been largely" cheaper-hydro energy access, not electrification-as-GPT (E-106), and it is localized to hydro-proximate counties with a slow national signal (E-107). This directly weakens reading the social-value stratum as broad, electrification-caused welfare.
- **Markups and uneven pass-through.** In concentrated markets, the measured productivity gain looks like higher markups rather than consumer pass-through, and the gains skewed to capital (negative labor share) (E-105, E-103) — so productivity value is not equivalent to diffuse social value.
- **Demand shock (vs overbuild).** The 1929–1932 utilization collapse is majority (≈55%) a Depression demand shock on fixed plant, with the remainder pre-1929 pipeline capacity; utilization materially recovered within the core window by 1935 (E-209), with further recovery visible in the 1937 post-window sensitivity (E-208, E-206) — the leading alternative to a physical-overbuild reading, and the one the evidence favors.
- **Market beta (vs financing structure).** The holding-tier drawdown is fully explained by static market beta with no index-level residual (E-307, E-306); at the index level the tier dispersion is a market-exposure difference, not demonstrably a financing-structure effect.
- **Survivorship and index construction.** The Cowles indexes are value-weighted, changing-composition, chained, and based to inception=100 per series (not 1926=100), so only within-series ratios are valid and survivorship is embedded — biasing measured holding-company loss *downward* (E-309, E-312).
- **Financing-efficiency interpretation.** A cost-of-capital / market-liquidity rationale exists for why the holding-company form emerged (S-014, via E-112) — a Weakens-candidate against a purely value-destructive reading of holding companies. It is **`Undecidable-pending-access`** (no realized-return series verified) and, by hard rule, is never entered as realized-return evidence.

## I. Pattern-Relevant Findings

**Descriptive input only.** Per `governance/PATTERN_VALIDATION_FRAMEWORK.md` §8 and the Gate 2 authorization, this section does **not** assign the formal cross-cycle labels Supports / Weakens / Rejects / Undecidable as verdicts. ### PAT-001 — Social Value / Investor Return Divergence
- **Evidence consistent with the mechanism:** a real, quantified industrial-electrification productivity signal (E-101, E-102) and a broad-economy MFP acceleration in the tail (E-108) **coexist** with weak/uneven and then sharply negative utility-securities returns, worst at the holding tier (E-114, E-115, E-301) — **coexisting divergence only, not a matched-cohort value transfer.** The US cycle presents a different institutional setting from HIS-001. Holding-company organization is a candidate explanation for part of the investor-outcome dispersion, but the current index-level and open-source evidence does not identify it as the causal mechanism (the index-tier dispersion is beta-absorbed, E-307; operating-company economics remain an open gap; the firm-level FTC evidence, S-005, is deferred).
- **Evidence against or narrowing it:** the social-value estimate is localized, slow nationally, partly cheaper-hydro, and markup-mediated (E-105, E-106, E-107); the two sides are different constructs on different units, so temporal overlap is not causal correspondence (E-113).
- **What remains undecidable:** the matched-cohort value-transfer question — whether the specific assets' value reached the specific investors — is Undecidable (E-118); and the Rejects test was executed and **not established** (E-117), i.e., it does not convert to a Supports finding. *(Fixed boundary 1: claim coexisting divergence only.)*

### PAT-002 — Capital-Cycle Overbuild
- **Evidence consistent with the mechanism:** a very large investor-owned capacity buildout (2.35× 1920→1929 — E-201, E-202) and a real 1929–1932 utilization collapse (−25.3% — E-207), with residual physical slack from capacity that kept rising into the demand collapse (E-204).
