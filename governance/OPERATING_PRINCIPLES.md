# Operating Principles

These principles govern how research is conducted and written, once scope and roles are settled elsewhere.

## Evidence Standards

### Priority Order

Evidence is preferred in this order, highest first:

1. Primary historical data (original records, filings, statistics as first published)
2. Company accounts, security prices, dividends, and failure records
3. Official statistics (government, central bank, national archives)
4. Academic economic history (peer-reviewed or university-press)
5. High-quality historical synthesis (reputable non-academic secondary works)
6. Popular books — orientation only, never load-bearing for a claim

Lower-priority sources may motivate a question. They must not, by themselves, support a claim classified as historical fact or quantitative estimate.

## Claim Classification

Every major claim in a research asset must be tagged as exactly one of:

- **historical fact** — directly documented and not seriously disputed
- **quantitative estimate** — a number derived or reconstructed, with a stated method and margin of uncertainty
- **scholarly interpretation** — a conclusion drawn by historians/economists, attributed to its source, not treated as settled fact
- **research inference** — a conclusion drawn by the repository's own analysis from the evidence assembled
- **AI-era analogy** — an explicit mapping from historical mechanism to the present AI cycle

Inferences and analogies must never be written, formatted, or cited as if they were established historical fact. The classification must be visible in the text (inline tag, table column, or explicit label), not merely implied.

## Mandatory Controls

Every research asset must include:

- **Counter-evidence**: for each major claim, the strongest available evidence against it, not only evidence for it.
- **Survivorship-bias check**: failed firms, impaired investors, bankrupt operators, and weak-return cases must be represented, not only the eventual winners.

A research asset that only documents winners is incomplete and must not be promoted past `draft`.

## Required Value Separation

The following concepts are analytically distinct and must never be collapsed into one another:

1. **Technological importance** — how significant the technical change was
2. **Social value creation** — aggregate economic/social benefit created
3. **Operating profit** — profit captured by operating businesses
4. **Bondholder return** — return earned by debt capital
5. **Shareholder return** — return earned by equity capital
6. **Long-term excess return** — return in excess of a relevant benchmark over time

A technology can be historically important and socially valuable while producing weak or negative returns for shareholders or bondholders who financed it. Every research asset touching value capture must address this distinction explicitly rather than assume "important technology" implies "good investment."

## Time Allocation

Across a research task, target allocation of effort is approximately:

- **Input (source gathering): ~30%**
- **Thinking (analysis, classification, cross-checking, counter-evidence): ~40%**
- **Output (writing the asset): ~30%**

Thinking time must not be compressed to expand output volume. A shorter, better-reasoned asset is preferred over a longer, thinly-reasoned one.

## Traceability and Falsifiability

- Every judgment, pattern, mapping, or thesis must be traceable to the evidence and reasoning that produced it (via the evidence ledger and source register).
- Every pattern, mapping, and thesis must state what evidence would falsify it. A claim that cannot in principle be falsified is not eligible for promotion beyond `draft`.
- Claims and their classifications may be revised; the revision and its reason are recorded, not silently overwritten.
